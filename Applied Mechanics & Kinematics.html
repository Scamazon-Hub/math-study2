Applied Mechanics & Kinematics

Engineering Analysis of Motion and Forces

Mechanics provides the fundamental principles for analyzing how objects move and interact under various forces. From structural engineering to vehicle dynamics, these concepts form the basis for designing and optimizing mechanical systems.

1. Newton's Laws of Motion

Foundation of Classical Mechanics

Newton's three laws describe the relationship between a body and the forces acting upon it, forming the basis for analyzing motion in engineering systems.

Fundamental Principles

Law Mathematical Expression Engineering Application
First Law (Inertia) \( \sum F = 0 \Rightarrow a = 0 \) Static equilibrium analysis
Second Law (Force & Acceleration) \( \sum F = ma \) Dynamic system analysis
Third Law (Action-Reaction) \( F_{AB} = -F_{BA} \) Force transmission in mechanisms

Example: Crate Acceleration

A net force of 200 N is applied to a 50 kg crate on a frictionless surface. Find the acceleration and distance traveled in 5 seconds.

Step 1: Apply Newton's Second Law
\( F = ma \Rightarrow a = \frac{F}{m} = \frac{200}{50} = 4 \text{ m/s}^2 \)

Step 2: Calculate displacement using SUVAT
\( s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2 = 0 + \frac{1}{2}(4)(5)^2 = 50 \text{ m} \)

Engineering Insight: The acceleration indicates how quickly the crate responds to the applied force, important for conveyor system design.

2. Equations of Motion (SUVAT)

Analyzing Motion with Constant Acceleration

The SUVAT equations provide a complete mathematical description of linear motion under constant acceleration, essential for projectile motion, vehicle dynamics, and mechanical systems.

Complete SUVAT Equation Set

\( v = u + at \)

\( s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2 \)

\( v^2 = u^2 + 2as \)

\( s = \frac{(u + v)}{2}t \)

\( s = vt - \frac{1}{2}at^2 \)

Variable Definitions

Symbol Quantity SI Unit
\( s \) Displacement meter (m)
\( u \) Initial velocity m/s
\( v \) Final velocity m/s
\( a \) Acceleration m/s²
\( t \) Time second (s)

Example: Projectile from Building

A ball is projected vertically upwards at 36 km/h from a 108 m tall building. Find the time taken to reach the ground (g = 9.8 m/s²).

Step 1: Convert units and define coordinate system
\( u = 36 \text{ km/h} = 10 \text{ m/s} \) (upwards positive)

Step 2: List known variables
\( s = -108 \text{ m} \) (final position below start)
\( u = +10 \text{ m/s} \)
\( a = -9.8 \text{ m/s}^2 \)
\( t = ? \)

Step 3: Apply appropriate equation
\( s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2 \)
\( -108 = 10t + \frac{1}{2}(-9.8)t^2 \)
\( -108 = 10t - 4.9t^2 \)

Step 4: Solve quadratic equation
\( 4.9t^2 - 10t - 108 = 0 \)
\( t = \frac{10 \pm \sqrt{(-10)^2 - 4(4.9)(-108)}}{2(4.9)} = \frac{10 \pm \sqrt{100 + 2116.8}}{9.8} = \frac{10 \pm 47.08}{9.8} \)
\( t = \frac{10 + 47.08}{9.8} \approx 5.82 \text{ s} \) (positive root selected)

Engineering Application: This calculation is essential for safety analysis in construction and demolition.

3. Circular Motion Dynamics

Analyzing Rotational Systems

Circular motion principles are fundamental for understanding rotating machinery, vehicle cornering, orbital mechanics, and centrifugal systems.

Key Relationships

Angular velocity: \( \omega = \frac{\theta}{t} = \frac{2\pi}{T} = 2\pi f \)

Linear velocity: \( v = r\omega \)

Centripetal acceleration: \( a_c = \frac{v^2}{r} = \omega^2 r \)

Centripetal force: \( F_c = \frac{mv^2}{r} = m\omega^2 r \)

Circular Motion Applications

Application Key Formula Engineering Significance
Banked curves \( \tan\theta = \frac{v^2}{rg} \) Road and track design
Conical pendulum \( \omega = \sqrt{\frac{g}{L\cos\theta}} \) Vibration analysis
Centrifugal governor \( \omega = \sqrt{\frac{g\tan\theta}{r}} \) Speed regulation

Example: Steam Engine Governor

A governor has two spheres on rods 60 cm long. Find the rotational speed in rpm when the rods make 20° with the vertical.

Step 1: Force balance equations
Horizontal: \( T\sin\theta = m\omega^2r \)
Vertical: \( T\cos\theta = mg \)
Dividing: \( \tan\theta = \frac{\omega^2r}{g} \)

Step 2: Geometry relationship
\( r = L\sin\theta = 0.6\sin(20^\circ) \approx 0.205 \text{ m} \)

Step 3: Solve for angular velocity
\( \tan(20^\circ) = \frac{\omega^2(0.205)}{9.81} \)
\( \omega^2 = \frac{9.81 \times \tan(20^\circ)}{0.205} \approx 17.4 \)
\( \omega \approx \sqrt{17.4} \approx 4.17 \text{ rad/s} \)

Step 4: Convert to rpm
\( \text{rpm} = 4.17 \times \frac{60}{2\pi} \approx 39.8 \text{ rpm} \)

Engineering Design: Governors prevent engine overspeed by regulating fuel supply based on centrifugal force.

4. Work, Energy, and Power

Energy Methods in Mechanical Analysis

Energy principles provide powerful alternative methods for analyzing mechanical systems, often simplifying complex force analyses.

Fundamental Energy Relationships

Work done: \( W = F \cdot d \cdot \cos\theta \)

Kinetic energy: \( KE = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 \)

Potential energy: \( PE = mgh \)

Power: \( P = \frac{W}{t} = F \cdot v \)

Work-energy theorem: \( W = \Delta KE \)

Conservation of mechanical energy: \( KE_i + PE_i = KE_f + PE_f \)

Energy Conversion Efficiency

System Typical Efficiency Energy Loss Factors
Electric motor 85-95% Heat, friction, magnetic losses
Internal combustion engine 25-40% Heat exhaust, friction, pumping
Mechanical transmission 90-98% Friction, vibration, heat

Example: Vehicle Acceleration

A 1200 kg car accelerates from 10 m/s to 30 m/s. Calculate the work done and average power if this takes 8 seconds.

Step 1: Apply work-energy principle
\( W = \Delta KE = \frac{1}{2}m(v^2 - u^2) \)
\( W = \frac{1}{2}(1200)(30^2 - 10^2) = 600(900 - 100) = 480,000 \text{ J} \)

Step 2: Calculate average power
\( P = \frac{W}{t} = \frac{480000}{8} = 60,000 \text{ W} = 60 \text{ kW} \)

Step 3: Consider real-world factors
Actual engine power required would be higher due to:
• Air resistance (increases with speed)
• Rolling resistance
• Transmission losses

Engineering Optimization: This analysis helps in engine sizing and transmission design.

5. Momentum and Impulse

Analyzing Collisions and Impacts

Momentum principles are essential for understanding collisions, explosions, and any situation where forces act over time intervals.

Momentum Fundamentals

Linear momentum: \( p = mv \)

Impulse: \( J = F\Delta t = \Delta p \)

Conservation of momentum: \( \sum p_{initial} = \sum p_{final} \)

Elastic collision: \( KE_{initial} = KE_{final} \)

Inelastic collision: \( KE_{initial} \neq KE_{final} \)

Example: Vehicle Collision Analysis

A 1500 kg car traveling at 20 m/s collides with a stationary 1000 kg car. If they stick together, find their final velocity.

Step 1: Apply conservation of momentum
\( m_1v_1 + m_2v_2 = (m_1 + m_2)v_f \)
\( (1500)(20) + (1000)(0) = (1500 + 1000)v_f \)
\( 30000 = 2500v_f \)

Step 2: Solve for final velocity
\( v_f = \frac{30000}{2500} = 12 \text{ m/s} \)

Step 3: Analyze energy loss
Initial KE: \( \frac{1}{2}(1500)(20)^2 = 300,000 \text{ J} \)
Final KE: \( \frac{1}{2}(2500)(12)^2 = 180,000 \text{ J} \)
Energy dissipated: \( 120,000 \text{ J} \) (40% loss)

Safety Engineering: This energy dissipation analysis informs crumple zone design in vehicles.

6. Friction and Inclined Planes

Real-World Force Interactions

Friction analysis is crucial for mechanical design, braking systems, traction control, and structural stability.

Friction Models

Static friction: \( F_s \leq \mu_s N \)

Kinetic friction: \( F_k = \mu_k N \)

Inclined plane (no friction): \( a = g\sin\theta \)

Inclined plane (with friction): \( a = g(\sin\theta - \mu\cos\theta) \)

Typical Friction Coefficients

Materials Static (μs) Kinetic (μk)
Steel on steel (dry) 0.6-0.8 0.4-0.6
Rubber on concrete (dry) 0.9-1.1 0.7-0.9
Ice on ice 0.05-0.15 0.02-0.10

Example: Inclined Plane with Friction

A 10 kg box rests on a 30° incline with μs = 0.4 and μk = 0.3. Determine if it will slide and its acceleration if it does.

Step 1: Calculate forces
Weight component parallel: \( mg\sin\theta = 10 \times 9.8 \times \sin(30^\circ) = 49 \text{ N} \)
Normal force: \( N = mg\cos\theta = 10 \times 9.8 \times \cos(30^\circ) \approx 84.9 \text{ N} \)
Maximum static friction: \( F_{s,max} = \mu_s N = 0.4 \times 84.9 \approx 33.96 \text{ N} \)

Step 2: Check for sliding
Since 49 N > 33.96 N, the box will slide

Step 3: Calculate acceleration
Kinetic friction: \( F_k = \mu_k N = 0.3 \times 84.9 \approx 25.47 \text{ N} \)
Net force: \( F_{net} = 49 - 25.47 = 23.53 \text{ N} \)
Acceleration: \( a = \frac{F_{net}}{m} = \frac{23.53}{10} \approx 2.35 \text{ m/s}^2 \)

Mechanical Design: This analysis informs ramp design and material selection for conveyor systems.

Engineering Practice: Mechanical analysis requires not just solving equations but understanding physical constraints, real-world factors like friction and air resistance, and safety considerations. Always validate mathematical results against engineering intuition and practical limitations.
