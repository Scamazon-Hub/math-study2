AC Circuit Analysis - Phasor Methods and Electrical System Analysis

AC Circuit Analysis

Engineering Application of Complex Numbers

Complex numbers provide an elegant mathematical framework for analyzing AC circuits. By representing sinusoidal quantities as phasors (complex numbers with magnitude and phase), we transform differential equations into algebraic equations, significantly simplifying circuit analysis.

1. AC Fundamentals and Waveform Characteristics

Sinusoidal Waveforms

Alternating Current (AC) systems are dominated by sinusoidal waveforms due to their mathematical properties and practical generation through rotating machinery. The general form of a sinusoidal voltage or current is:
$$ v(t) = V_m\sin(\omega t + \phi) \quad \text{or} \quad v(t) = V_m\cos(\omega t + \phi) $$

Key AC Parameters

Parameter Symbol Formula Description
Frequency \( f \) \( f = \frac{\omega}{2\pi} \) Number of cycles per second (Hz)
Period \( T \) \( T = \frac{1}{f} = \frac{2\pi}{\omega} \) Time for one complete cycle (seconds)
Angular Frequency \( \omega \) \( \omega = 2\pi f \) Radians per second
Peak Value \( V_m \) or \( I_m \) Maximum amplitude Maximum instantaneous value
RMS Value \( V_{rms} \) or \( I_{rms} \) \( V_{rms} = \frac{V_m}{\sqrt{2}} \) DC equivalent for power calculation
Peak-to-Peak \( V_{pp} \) or \( I_{pp} \) \( V_{pp} = 2V_m \) Difference between maximum and minimum
Engineering Note: Unless specified otherwise, AC voltages and currents are typically quoted as RMS values. For example, "230V AC" means 230V RMS, with a peak value of approximately 325V.

Example: Complete AC Waveform Analysis

An AC voltage is given by \( v(t) = 120\sin(100\pi t + \frac{\pi}{4}) \) volts. Determine all key parameters.

Solution:

a) Peak Voltage: \( V_m = 120 \) V

b) RMS Voltage: \( V_{rms} = \frac{120}{\sqrt{2}} \approx 84.85 \) V

c) Angular Frequency: \( \omega = 100\pi \approx 314.16 \) rad/s

d) Frequency: \( f = \frac{\omega}{2\pi} = \frac{100\pi}{2\pi} = 50 \) Hz

e) Period: \( T = \frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{50} = 0.02 \) s = 20 ms

f) Phase Angle: \( \phi = \frac{\pi}{4} = 45^\circ \) (voltage leads reference)

g) Instantaneous voltage at t=10ms: \( v(0.01) = 120\sin(100\pi \times 0.01 + \frac{\pi}{4}) = 120\sin(\pi + \frac{\pi}{4}) = 120\sin(\frac{5\pi}{4}) \approx -84.85 \) V

2. Phasor Representation of AC Quantities

Phasor Domain Transformation

Phasors convert time-domain sinusoidal functions into complex numbers in the frequency domain. This transformation eliminates the time variable, simplifying analysis.

Phasor Conversion

A sinusoidal function \( v(t) = V_m\cos(\omega t + \phi) \) is represented as a phasor \( \mathbf{V} = V_{rms} \angle \phi \).

$$ v(t) = V_m\cos(\omega t + \phi) \quad \Leftrightarrow \quad \mathbf{V} = V_{rms} \angle \phi = V_{rms} e^{j\phi} $$

Complex Number Forms

Form Expression Conversion
Rectangular \( a + jb \) \( a = |Z|\cos\theta, \quad b = |Z|\sin\theta \)
Polar \( |Z| \angle \theta \) \( |Z| = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}, \quad \theta = \tan^{-1}(\frac{b}{a}) \)
Exponential \( |Z| e^{j\theta} \) Euler's formula: \( e^{j\theta} = \cos\theta + j\sin\theta \)

Example: Phasor Operations

Convert the following time-domain functions to phasor form:

a) \( i_1(t) = 10\cos(377t + 30^\circ) \) A

b) \( v_2(t) = 5\sin(100\pi t - 45^\circ) \) V (convert to cosine first)

Solution:

a) \( I_1 = \frac{10}{\sqrt{2}} \angle 30^\circ \approx 7.07 \angle 30^\circ \) A

b) First convert to cosine: \( \sin(\theta) = \cos(\theta - 90^\circ) \)

\( v_2(t) = 5\cos(100\pi t - 45^\circ - 90^\circ) = 5\cos(100\pi t - 135^\circ) \)

\( V_2 = \frac{5}{\sqrt{2}} \angle -135^\circ \approx 3.54 \angle -135^\circ \) V

3. Impedance and Admittance

Generalized Resistance

Impedance (Z) extends the concept of resistance to AC circuits, accounting for both magnitude and phase relationships. Admittance (Y) is the reciprocal of impedance, particularly useful for parallel circuit analysis.

Component Impedance Characteristics

Component Impedance (Z) Magnitude Phase Angle Power Dissipation
Resistor (R) \( Z_R = R \) \( R \) \( 0^\circ \) Dissipates real power
Inductor (L) \( Z_L = j\omega L \) \( \omega L \) \( +90^\circ \) Stores energy in magnetic field
Capacitor (C) \( Z_C = \frac{1}{j\omega C} = -j\frac{1}{\omega C} \) \( \frac{1}{\omega C} \) \( -90^\circ \) Stores energy in electric field
Memory Aid: "ELI the ICE man" - E (voltage) leads I (current) in L (inductor), I leads E in C (capacitor).

Example: Series RLC Circuit Analysis

A series RLC circuit has R = 100Ω, L = 0.5H, C = 10μF, connected to a 50Hz, 230V RMS supply. Calculate the total impedance, current, and voltage across each component.

Solution:

Step 1: Calculate angular frequency
\( \omega = 2\pi f = 2\pi(50) = 100\pi \approx 314.16 \) rad/s

Step 2: Calculate individual impedances
\( Z_R = 100 \) Ω
\( Z_L = j\omega L = j(314.16)(0.5) = j157.08 \) Ω
\( Z_C = -j\frac{1}{\omega C} = -j\frac{1}{314.16 \times 10 \times 10^{-6}} = -j318.31 \) Ω

Step 3: Total impedance
\( Z_{total} = Z_R + Z_L + Z_C = 100 + j157.08 - j318.31 = 100 - j161.23 \) Ω

Magnitude: \( |Z| = \sqrt{100^2 + (-161.23)^2} \approx 189.47 \) Ω

Phase: \( \theta = \tan^{-1}(\frac{-161.23}{100}) \approx -58.17^\circ \)

Step 4: Circuit current
\( I = \frac{V}{Z} = \frac{230\angle 0^\circ}{189.47\angle -58.17^\circ} \approx 1.214\angle 58.17^\circ \) A

Step 5: Component voltages
\( V_R = I \times R = 1.214\angle 58.17^\circ \times 100 \approx 121.4\angle 58.17^\circ \) V
\( V_L = I \times Z_L = 1.214\angle 58.17^\circ \times 157.08\angle 90^\circ \approx 190.7\angle 148.17^\circ \) V
\( V_C = I \times Z_C = 1.214\angle 58.17^\circ \times 318.31\angle -90^\circ \approx 386.4\angle -31.83^\circ \) V

4. AC Circuit Analysis Methods

Systematic Approaches

Standard circuit analysis techniques (Ohm's Law, Kirchhoff's Laws, mesh and nodal analysis) apply to AC circuits when using phasors and complex impedances.

Key Analysis Techniques

Method Application Key Equations
Ohm's Law (AC) Basic circuit relationships \( \mathbf{V} = \mathbf{I} \cdot \mathbf{Z} \)
Kirchhoff's Voltage Law Series circuits \( \sum \mathbf{V} = 0 \) around any closed loop
Kirchhoff's Current Law Parallel circuits \( \sum \mathbf{I} = 0 \) at any node
Mesh Analysis Multiple loop circuits Solve simultaneous equations for mesh currents
Nodal Analysis Multiple node circuits Solve simultaneous equations for node voltages
Superposition Multiple source circuits Analyze each source independently, sum results

Example: Mesh Analysis of AC Circuit

Analyze the circuit with two voltage sources: \( V_1 = 100\angle 0^\circ \) V, \( V_2 = 50\angle 90^\circ \) V, impedances: \( Z_1 = 4 + j3 \) Ω, \( Z_2 = 2 - j5 \) Ω, \( Z_3 = 3 + j4 \) Ω.

Solution:

Step 1: Define mesh currents
Let \( I_1 \) flow clockwise in left mesh, \( I_2 \) in right mesh.

Step 2: Write mesh equations
Mesh 1: \( (Z_1 + Z_3)I_1 - Z_3I_2 = V_1 \)
Mesh 2: \( -Z_3I_1 + (Z_2 + Z_3)I_2 = -V_2 \)

Step 3: Substitute values
\( (4+j3 + 3+j4)I_1 - (3+j4)I_2 = 100\angle 0^\circ \)
\( -(3+j4)I_1 + (2-j5 + 3+j4)I_2 = -50\angle 90^\circ \)

Simplify: \( (7+j7)I_1 - (3+j4)I_2 = 100 \)
\( -(3+j4)I_1 + (5-j1)I_2 = -j50 \)

Step 4: Solve the system
Using determinants or matrix methods:
\( I_1 \approx 12.56\angle -26.57^\circ \) A
\( I_2 \approx 8.94\angle 116.57^\circ \) A

5. Power Analysis in AC Circuits

Complex Power Concepts

AC power analysis involves real power (active), reactive power, and apparent power, with the power factor indicating efficiency.

Power Relationships

Power Type Symbol Formula Unit
Real Power (Active) P \( P = V_{rms}I_{rms}\cos\theta \) Watts (W)
Reactive Power Q \( Q = V_{rms}I_{rms}\sin\theta \) Volt-Ampere Reactive (VAR)
Apparent Power S \( S = V_{rms}I_{rms} \) Volt-Ampere (VA)
Complex Power S \( \mathbf{S} = P + jQ \) VA
Power Factor PF \( PF = \cos\theta = \frac{P}{S} \) Dimensionless

Example: Power Calculation in RLC Circuit

For the series RLC circuit analyzed earlier (R=100Ω, L=0.5H, C=10μF, V=230V RMS, f=50Hz), calculate all power quantities.

Solution:

From previous analysis: \( I = 1.214\angle 58.17^\circ \) A, \( \theta = 58.17^\circ \)

Step 1: Apparent Power
\( S = V_{rms}I_{rms} = 230 \times 1.214 = 279.22 \) VA

Step 2: Real Power
\( P = VI\cos\theta = 230 \times 1.214 \times \cos(58.17^\circ) \approx 147.5 \) W

Alternative: \( P = I^2R = (1.214)^2 \times 100 \approx 147.4 \) W

Step 3: Reactive Power
\( Q = VI\sin\theta = 230 \times 1.214 \times \sin(58.17^\circ) \approx 237.8 \) VAR

Step 4: Complex Power
\( \mathbf{S} = P + jQ = 147.5 + j237.8 \) VA

Magnitude: \( |\mathbf{S}| = \sqrt{147.5^2 + 237.8^2} \approx 279.2 \) VA (matches apparent power)

Step 5: Power Factor
\( PF = \cos\theta = \cos(58.17^\circ) \approx 0.528 \) (lagging, since current lags voltage)

6. Resonance in AC Circuits

Frequency-Selective Behavior

Resonance occurs when the inductive and capacitive reactances cancel each other, resulting in purely resistive impedance at a specific frequency.

Series and Parallel Resonance

Parameter Series Resonance Parallel Resonance
Resonant Frequency \( f_r = \frac{1}{2\pi\sqrt{LC}} \) \( f_r = \frac{1}{2\pi\sqrt{LC}} \)
Impedance at Resonance Minimum (Z = R) Maximum (Z ≈ R)
Current at Resonance Maximum Minimum
Quality Factor (Q) \( Q = \frac{\omega_r L}{R} = \frac{1}{\omega_r CR} \) \( Q = \frac{R}{\omega_r L} = \omega_r CR \)
Bandwidth \( BW = \frac{f_r}{Q} \) \( BW = \frac{f_r}{Q} \)

Example: Series Resonance Analysis

A series RLC circuit has R = 10Ω, L = 100mH, C = 10μF. Calculate the resonant frequency, quality factor, and bandwidth.

Solution:

Step 1: Resonant Frequency
\( f_r = \frac{1}{2\pi\sqrt{LC}} = \frac{1}{2\pi\sqrt{0.1 \times 10 \times 10^{-6}}} \approx 159.15 \) Hz

Step 2: Quality Factor
\( \omega_r = 2\pi f_r = 2\pi \times 159.15 \approx 1000 \) rad/s
\( Q = \frac{\omega_r L}{R} = \frac{1000 \times 0.1}{10} = 10 \)

Step 3: Bandwidth
\( BW = \frac{f_r}{Q} = \frac{159.15}{10} = 15.915 \) Hz

Step 4: Cutoff Frequencies
Lower cutoff: \( f_1 = f_r - \frac{BW}{2} = 159.15 - 7.9575 = 151.19 \) Hz
Upper cutoff: \( f_2 = f_r + \frac{BW}{2} = 159.15 + 7.9575 = 167.11 \) Hz

7. Three-Phase AC Systems

Power Distribution Standard

Three-phase systems are the standard for power generation, transmission, and distribution due to their efficiency and power delivery characteristics.

Three-Phase Configurations

Configuration Voltage Relationship Current Relationship Power Formula
Wye (Y) Connection \( V_L = \sqrt{3}V_P \) \( I_L = I_P \) \( P = \sqrt{3}V_LI_L\cos\theta \)
Delta (Δ) Connection \( V_L = V_P \) \( I_L = \sqrt{3}I_P \) \( P = \sqrt{3}V_LI_L\cos\theta \)

Example: Three-Phase Power Calculation

A balanced three-phase Y-connected load has per-phase impedance of \( 10 + j5 \) Ω and is connected to a 400V line-to-line supply. Calculate phase and line currents, and total power consumed.

Solution:

Step 1: Phase Voltage
For Y-connection: \( V_P = \frac{V_L}{\sqrt{3}} = \frac{400}{\sqrt{3}} \approx 230.94 \) V

Step 2: Phase Impedance
\( Z_P = 10 + j5 \) Ω
Magnitude: \( |Z_P| = \sqrt{10^2 + 5^2} \approx 11.18 \) Ω
Phase angle: \( \theta = \tan^{-1}(\frac{5}{10}) = 26.57^\circ \)

Step 3: Phase Current
\( I_P = \frac{V_P}{|Z_P|} = \frac{230.94}{11.18} \approx 20.66 \) A

Step 4: Line Current
For Y-connection: \( I_L = I_P = 20.66 \) A

Step 5: Total Power
\( P_{total} = 3 \times V_PI_P\cos\theta = 3 \times 230.94 \times 20.66 \times \cos(26.57^\circ) \approx 12,800 \) W
Alternative: \( P_{total} = \sqrt{3}V_LI_L\cos\theta = \sqrt{3} \times 400 \times 20.66 \times \cos(26.57^\circ) \approx 12,800 \) W

Engineering Analysis: AC circuit analysis using phasor methods is fundamental to electrical engineering. The ability to convert between time and frequency domains, analyze impedance characteristics, and calculate power relationships is essential for designing and troubleshooting electrical systems.
