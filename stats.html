Statistics - Data Analysis for Engineering Decision Making

Statistics for Engineering

Data-Driven Engineering Decisions

Statistics provides the mathematical foundation for analyzing data, making predictions, and drawing conclusions from uncertain information. In engineering, statistical methods are essential for quality control, reliability analysis, experimental design, and risk assessment.

1. Descriptive Statistics

Summarizing Engineering Data

Descriptive statistics provide tools to summarize and understand the key characteristics of engineering datasets, including central tendency, variability, and distribution shape.

Measures of Central Tendency

These values represent the "center" or typical value of a dataset:

Measure Formula Engineering Application When to Use
Mean (Arithmetic Average) \( \bar{x} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i}{n} \) Average strength of materials Normally distributed data without outliers
Median (Middle Value) Middle value when data sorted Typical response time in control systems Skewed distributions or with outliers
Mode (Most Frequent) Value with highest frequency Most common failure mode Categorical or discrete data

Measures of Dispersion

These quantify how spread out the data values are:

Range: \( R = x_{max} - x_{min} \)

Variance (Population): \( \sigma^2 = \frac{\sum (x_i - \mu)^2}{N} \)

Variance (Sample): \( s^2 = \frac{\sum (x_i - \bar{x})^2}{n-1} \)

Standard Deviation: \( \sigma = \sqrt{\sigma^2} \) or \( s = \sqrt{s^2} \)

Coefficient of Variation: \( CV = \frac{\sigma}{\mu} \times 100\% \)

Example: Quality Control Analysis

A manufacturing process produces components with the following diameters (mm): {24.8, 25.2, 25.1, 24.9, 25.3, 25.0, 24.7, 25.4, 25.1, 24.8}

Step 1: Calculate Mean
\( \bar{x} = \frac{24.8 + 25.2 + 25.1 + 24.9 + 25.3 + 25.0 + 24.7 + 25.4 + 25.1 + 24.8}{10} = 25.03 \) mm

Step 2: Calculate Variance and Standard Deviation
\( s^2 = \frac{(24.8-25.03)^2 + (25.2-25.03)^2 + \cdots + (24.8-25.03)^2}{9} = 0.054 \) mm²
\( s = \sqrt{0.054} = 0.232 \) mm

Step 3: Calculate Coefficient of Variation
\( CV = \frac{0.232}{25.03} \times 100\% = 0.93\% \)

Engineering Interpretation: The manufacturing process has low variability (CV < 1%), indicating good process control.

2. Probability Theory

Quantifying Uncertainty in Engineering

Probability provides the mathematical language for dealing with uncertainty, which is inherent in all engineering systems and measurements.

Fundamental Probability Rules

Addition Rule: \( P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B) \)

Multiplication Rule (Independent Events): \( P(A \cap B) = P(A) \cdot P(B) \)

Conditional Probability: \( P(A|B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)} \)

Bayes' Theorem: \( P(A|B) = \frac{P(B|A) \cdot P(A)}{P(B)} \)

Probability Distributions

Distribution Probability Function Engineering Application
Binomial \( P(X=k) = \binom{n}{k} p^k (1-p)^{n-k} \) Quality control, defect analysis
Poisson \( P(X=k) = \frac{\lambda^k e^{-\lambda}}{k!} \) Failure rates, arrival processes
Exponential \( f(x) = \lambda e^{-\lambda x} \) Time between failures, reliability

Example: Reliability Analysis

A system has three independent components with reliability probabilities: P(A) = 0.95, P(B) = 0.98, P(C) = 0.92. The system fails if any component fails.

Step 1: Calculate System Reliability
Since components are independent and system fails if any fails:
\( P(System\ Works) = P(A) \cdot P(B) \cdot P(C) = 0.95 \times 0.98 \times 0.92 = 0.857 \)

Step 2: Calculate System Failure Probability
\( P(System\ Fails) = 1 - P(System\ Works) = 1 - 0.857 = 0.143 \)

Engineering Interpretation: There is a 14.3% probability of system failure, which may be unacceptable for critical applications.

3. Normal Distribution & Applications

The Foundation of Statistical Inference

The normal distribution is fundamental to engineering statistics due to the Central Limit Theorem, which states that means of samples tend toward normality regardless of the underlying distribution.

Standard Normal Distribution

Any normal distribution can be transformed to the standard normal distribution (μ=0, σ=1) using the Z-score:

$$ Z = \frac{X - \mu}{\sigma} $$

Empirical Rule (68-95-99.7 Rule)

For normally distributed data:

  • Approximately 68% of values fall within ±1σ of the mean
  • Approximately 95% of values fall within ±2σ of the mean
  • Approximately 99.7% of values fall within ±3σ of the mean

Example: Tolerance Analysis

A manufactured shaft has diameter normally distributed with μ = 25.00 mm and σ = 0.05 mm. Specifications require 24.90 mm to 25.10 mm.

Step 1: Calculate Z-scores for specification limits
Lower limit: \( Z_L = \frac{24.90 - 25.00}{0.05} = -2.0 \)
Upper limit: \( Z_U = \frac{25.10 - 25.00}{0.05} = 2.0 \)

Step 2: Find probabilities using standard normal table
\( P(Z < -2.0) = 0.0228 \)
\( P(Z > 2.0) = 0.0228 \)
Total outside specifications: 0.0228 + 0.0228 = 0.0456

Step 3: Calculate percentage within specifications
\( P(within\ specs) = 1 - 0.0456 = 0.9544 \) or 95.44%

Engineering Interpretation: Approximately 4.56% of shafts will be outside specifications, which may require process improvement.

4. Statistical Inference

Drawing Conclusions from Sample Data

Statistical inference allows engineers to make decisions about populations based on sample data, including estimation and hypothesis testing.

Confidence Intervals

Range of values that likely contains the population parameter:

Mean (σ known): \( \bar{x} \pm z_{\alpha/2} \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} \)

Mean (σ unknown): \( \bar{x} \pm t_{\alpha/2, n-1} \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}} \)

Proportion: \( \hat{p} \pm z_{\alpha/2} \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{n}} \)

Hypothesis Testing

Procedure for testing claims about population parameters:

  1. State null (H₀) and alternative (H₁) hypotheses
  2. Select significance level (α, typically 0.05)
  3. Calculate test statistic
  4. Determine critical value or p-value
  5. Make decision and interpret results

Example: Material Strength Test

A new alloy is claimed to have mean tensile strength > 400 MPa. A sample of 25 specimens gives \(\bar{x} = 405\) MPa with s = 15 MPa. Test at α = 0.05.

Step 1: State hypotheses
H₀: μ ≤ 400 MPa (alloy does not meet specification)
H₁: μ > 400 MPa (alloy meets specification)

Step 2: Calculate test statistic
\( t = \frac{\bar{x} - \mu_0}{s/\sqrt{n}} = \frac{405 - 400}{15/\sqrt{25}} = \frac{5}{3} = 1.667 \)

Step 3: Determine critical value
For one-tailed test with α = 0.05 and df = 24, t-critical = 1.711

Step 4: Make decision
Since t = 1.667 < 1.711, we fail to reject H₀

Engineering Interpretation: There is insufficient evidence to conclude that the alloy meets the strength specification at the 95% confidence level.

5. Regression Analysis

Modeling Relationships Between Variables

Regression analysis helps engineers understand and quantify relationships between variables, enabling prediction and optimization.

Simple Linear Regression

Model: \( y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x + \varepsilon \)

Slope: \( b_1 = \frac{\sum (x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y})}{\sum (x_i - \bar{x})^2} = \frac{S_{xy}}{S_{xx}} \)

Intercept: \( b_0 = \bar{y} - b_1 \bar{x} \)

Coefficient of Determination: \( R^2 = \frac{SSR}{SST} = 1 - \frac{SSE}{SST} \)

Correlation Analysis

Pearson correlation coefficient measures linear relationship strength:

$$ r = \frac{\sum (x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y})}{\sqrt{\sum (x_i - \bar{x})^2 \sum (y_i - \bar{y})^2}} = \frac{S_{xy}}{\sqrt{S_{xx} S_{yy}}} $$

Example: Temperature vs. Power Consumption

An engineer collects data on ambient temperature (x, °C) and power consumption (y, kW) of a cooling system:

Temperature (°C)2022252830
Power (kW)15.216.117.819.520.6

Step 1: Calculate summary statistics
\( \bar{x} = 25, \quad \bar{y} = 17.84 \)
\( S_{xx} = 104, \quad S_{yy} = 22.352, \quad S_{xy} = 47.6 \)

Step 2: Calculate regression coefficients
\( b_1 = \frac{47.6}{104} = 0.4577 \)
\( b_0 = 17.84 - 0.4577 \times 25 = 6.3975 \)

Step 3: Calculate correlation coefficient
\( r = \frac{47.6}{\sqrt{104 \times 22.352}} = 0.993 \)

Regression Equation: \( \hat{y} = 6.40 + 0.458x \)

Engineering Interpretation: Strong positive correlation (r ≈ 0.993) indicates power consumption increases with temperature. The model explains 98.6% of variation (R² = 0.986).

6. Statistical Quality Control

Monitoring and Improving Processes

Statistical quality control uses statistical methods to monitor and control processes to ensure they operate at their full potential.

Control Charts

Tools for monitoring process variation over time:

X-bar Chart (Means): Centerline = \(\bar{\bar{x}}\), UCL/LCL = \(\bar{\bar{x}} \pm A_2 \bar{R}\)

R Chart (Ranges): Centerline = \(\bar{R}\), UCL = \(D_4 \bar{R}\), LCL = \(D_3 \bar{R}\)

p Chart (Proportion Defective): Centerline = \(\bar{p}\), UCL/LCL = \(\bar{p} \pm 3\sqrt{\frac{\bar{p}(1-\bar{p})}{n}}\)

Process Capability Analysis

Measures how well a process meets specifications:

Cp (Potential Capability): \( C_p = \frac{USL - LSL}{6\sigma} \)

Cpk (Actual Capability): \( C_{pk} = \min\left(\frac{USL - \mu}{3\sigma}, \frac{\mu - LSL}{3\sigma}\right) \)

Example: Process Capability Assessment

A machining process produces components with specification 50.0 ± 0.2 mm. Process data shows μ = 50.05 mm and σ = 0.06 mm.

Step 1: Calculate specification limits
USL = 50.2 mm, LSL = 49.8 mm

Step 2: Calculate process capability indices
\( C_p = \frac{50.2 - 49.8}{6 \times 0.06} = \frac{0.4}{0.36} = 1.11 \)
\( C_{pk} = \min\left(\frac{50.2 - 50.05}{3 \times 0.06}, \frac{50.05 - 49.8}{3 \times 0.06}\right) = \min(0.83, 1.39) = 0.83 \)

Engineering Interpretation: The process is potentially capable (Cp > 1) but not centered (Cpk < Cp). The process should be adjusted to center it within specifications.

Engineering Practice: Statistical thinking is not just about calculations but about understanding variation and its impact on engineering systems. Always consider the practical significance of statistical findings in addition to their statistical significance.
