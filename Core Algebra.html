Core Algebra

Algebraic Foundation for Engineering

Algebra provides the fundamental language and tools for solving engineering problems. From circuit analysis to structural calculations, algebraic manipulation is essential for deriving solutions and understanding relationships between variables.

1. Quadratic Equations & Applications

Solving Second-Degree Polynomials

Quadratic equations appear frequently in engineering calculations involving projectile motion, optimization problems, AC circuit analysis, and mechanical systems.

Quadratic Formula

For any quadratic equation in the form \( ax^2 + bx + c = 0 \), the solutions are given by:

$$ x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a} $$

Discriminant Analysis

The discriminant \( \Delta = b^2 - 4ac \) determines the nature of the roots:

Discriminant Root Type Engineering Interpretation
\( \Delta > 0 \) Two distinct real roots System has two distinct operating points
\( \Delta = 0 \) One real repeated root Critical damping or optimal solution
\( \Delta < 0 \) Complex conjugate roots Oscillatory behavior or instability

Example: Projectile Motion

A projectile is launched with initial velocity \( v_0 = 50 \) m/s at angle \( \theta = 30^\circ \). Find when it hits the ground.

Step 1: Vertical motion equation
\( y = v_0 \sin\theta \cdot t - \frac{1}{2}gt^2 \)
\( y = 50 \cdot \sin30^\circ \cdot t - \frac{1}{2}(9.8)t^2 \)
\( y = 25t - 4.9t^2 \)

Step 2: Set y = 0 and solve
\( 0 = 25t - 4.9t^2 \)
\( t(25 - 4.9t) = 0 \)
Solutions: \( t = 0 \) (launch) and \( t = \frac{25}{4.9} \approx 5.10 \) seconds

Engineering Insight: The positive root gives the time of flight.

2. Polynomial Theory & Applications

Remainder and Factor Theorems

Polynomial Division Shortcuts

These theorems provide efficient methods for analyzing polynomial behavior without performing full polynomial division.

Remainder Theorem: If polynomial \( P(x) \) is divided by \( (x - a) \), the remainder is \( P(a) \)

Factor Theorem: \( (x - a) \) is a factor of \( P(x) \) if and only if \( P(a) = 0 \)

Example: Factor Analysis

Determine if \( (x - 3) \) is a factor of \( P(x) = x^3 - 6x^2 + 11x - 6 \)

Step 1: Evaluate P(3)
\( P(3) = 3^3 - 6(3)^2 + 11(3) - 6 = 27 - 54 + 33 - 6 = 0 \)

Step 2: Apply Factor Theorem
Since \( P(3) = 0 \), \( (x - 3) \) is a factor.

Step 3: Polynomial Division
\( \frac{x^3 - 6x^2 + 11x - 6}{x - 3} = x^2 - 3x + 2 \)

Complete Factorization: \( (x - 3)(x - 1)(x - 2) \)

3. Sequences and Series

Mathematical Patterns in Engineering

Sequences and series model growth patterns, signal processing, financial calculations, and many other engineering phenomena.

Arithmetic Progression (AP)

Constant difference between terms: \( a, a+d, a+2d, \ldots \)

n-th term: \( T_n = a + (n-1)d \)

Sum of n terms: \( S_n = \frac{n}{2}[2a + (n-1)d] = \frac{n}{2}(a + l) \)

Geometric Progression (GP)

Constant ratio between terms: \( a, ar, ar^2, \ldots \)

n-th term: \( T_n = ar^{n-1} \)

Sum of n terms: \( S_n = \frac{a(1-r^n)}{1-r} \) for \( r \neq 1 \)

Sum to infinity: \( S_\infty = \frac{a}{1-r} \) for \( |r| < 1 \)

Example: Compound Interest

£1000 invested at 5% annual interest compounded yearly. Find value after 10 years.

Step 1: Identify as GP
Initial: \( a = 1000 \), ratio \( r = 1.05 \)

Step 2: Apply GP formula
\( T_{11} = 1000 \times (1.05)^{10} \)

Step 3: Calculate
\( T_{11} = 1000 \times 1.62889 \approx £1628.89 \)

4. Complex Numbers in Engineering

Essential for AC Circuit Analysis

Complex numbers provide a powerful framework for analyzing sinusoidal signals, representing both magnitude and phase information simultaneously.

Complex Number Forms

Form Representation Engineering Use
Rectangular \( z = a + bi \) Component analysis
Polar \( z = r(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta) \) Magnitude and phase
Exponential \( z = re^{i\theta} \) Signal processing

Operations and Conversions

Example: AC Circuit Analysis

Given impedance \( Z_1 = 3 + 4j \) Ω and \( Z_2 = 1 - 2j \) Ω in series:

Step 1: Total impedance
\( Z_{total} = Z_1 + Z_2 = (3+4j) + (1-2j) = 4 + 2j \) Ω

Step 2: Convert to polar form
Magnitude: \( |Z| = \sqrt{4^2 + 2^2} = \sqrt{20} \approx 4.47 \) Ω
Phase: \( \theta = \tan^{-1}(\frac{2}{4}) = \tan^{-1}(0.5) \approx 26.57^\circ \)

Engineering Interpretation: The circuit has impedance 4.47 Ω with phase angle 26.57°

5. Systems of Equations

Solving Multiple Constraints

Engineering systems often involve multiple variables with interdependent relationships. Matrix methods provide efficient solutions.

Matrix Methods

For system \( AX = B \):

Inverse Method: \( X = A^{-1}B \)

Cramer's Rule: \( x_i = \frac{\det(A_i)}{\det(A)} \)

Example: Circuit Analysis

Solve for currents in a 3-mesh circuit:

\( 5I_1 - 2I_2 = 10 \)
\( -2I_1 + 8I_2 - 3I_3 = 0 \)
\( -3I_2 + 6I_3 = 5 \)

Matrix Solution:
\( \begin{bmatrix} 5 & -2 & 0 \\ -2 & 8 & -3 \\ 0 & -3 & 6 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} I_1 \\ I_2 \\ I_3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 10 \\ 0 \\ 5 \end{bmatrix} \)

Solution yields: \( I_1 \approx 2.15A, I_2 \approx 0.88A, I_3 \approx 1.27A \)

Engineering Practice: Algebraic proficiency is not just about solving equations but understanding what the solutions mean in physical terms. Always interpret mathematical results in the context of the engineering problem.
