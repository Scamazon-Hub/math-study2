Trigonometry - Angular Relationships in Engineering Systems

Trigonometry for Engineering

Modeling Periodic Phenomena and Angular Relationships

Trigonometry provides the mathematical foundation for analyzing periodic behavior, rotational motion, wave phenomena, and spatial relationships essential in mechanical, electrical, and civil engineering applications.

1. Right-Angled Triangle Fundamentals

Basis for Spatial Analysis

Right-angled triangle trigonometry forms the foundation for analyzing forces, distances, and angles in structural design, navigation, and mechanical systems.

Pythagorean Theorem

Fundamental relationship between sides of a right triangle:

$$ a^2 + b^2 = c^2 $$

Where \( c \) is the hypotenuse, and \( a \), \( b \) are the other two sides.

Trigonometric Ratios

SOH CAH TOA Mnemonic:

$$ \sin\theta = \frac{\text{Opposite}}{\text{Hypotenuse}} $$

$$ \cos\theta = \frac{\text{Adjacent}}{\text{Hypotenuse}} $$

$$ \tan\theta = \frac{\text{Opposite}}{\text{Adjacent}} $$

Function Engineering Application Typical Use Case
Sine (sin) Vertical components of forces Structural load analysis
Cosine (cos) Horizontal components of forces Mechanical equilibrium
Tangent (tan) Slope calculations Inclined plane problems

Example: Force Resolution

A force of 500 N is applied at 30° to the horizontal. Find the horizontal and vertical components.

Step 1: Horizontal Component (Adjacent)
\( F_x = F \cos\theta = 500 \times \cos30^\circ = 500 \times 0.866 = 433.0 \text{ N} \)

Step 2: Vertical Component (Opposite)
\( F_y = F \sin\theta = 500 \times \sin30^\circ = 500 \times 0.5 = 250.0 \text{ N} \)

Engineering Interpretation: The force has 433 N pushing horizontally and 250 N lifting vertically.

2. Radians and Circular Motion

The Natural Angular Measure

Radians provide the most natural unit for angular measurement in calculus and physics, simplifying derivatives and integrals of trigonometric functions.

Angle Conversion

Degrees to Radians: \( \theta_{rad} = \theta_{deg} \times \frac{\pi}{180} \)

Radians to Degrees: \( \theta_{deg} = \theta_{rad} \times \frac{180}{\pi} \)

Circular Geometry

Arc Length: \( s = r\theta \) (θ in radians)

Sector Area: \( A = \frac{1}{2}r^2\theta \) (θ in radians)

Rotational Kinematics

Angular Velocity: \( \omega = \frac{d\theta}{dt} \)

Linear Velocity: \( v = r\omega \)

Centripetal Acceleration: \( a_c = \omega^2 r = \frac{v^2}{r} \)

Centripetal Force: \( F_c = m\omega^2 r = \frac{mv^2}{r} \)

Example: Flywheel Design

A flywheel rotates at 3000 RPM with diameter 0.5 m. Calculate angular velocity, linear speed at rim, and centripetal acceleration for a 1 kg mass at the rim.

Step 1: Convert RPM to rad/s
\( \omega = 3000 \times \frac{2\pi}{60} = 3000 \times 0.10472 = 314.16 \text{ rad/s} \)

Step 2: Calculate linear speed
\( v = r\omega = 0.25 \times 314.16 = 78.54 \text{ m/s} \)

Step 3: Calculate centripetal acceleration
\( a_c = \omega^2 r = (314.16)^2 \times 0.25 = 24674 \text{ m/s}^2 \)

Step 4: Calculate centripetal force
\( F_c = ma_c = 1 \times 24674 = 24674 \text{ N} \)

Engineering Interpretation: The flywheel rim experiences enormous forces equivalent to 2500g acceleration.

3. Non-Right Triangle Analysis

Solving General Triangles

The sine and cosine rules extend trigonometric analysis to any triangle, essential for triangulation, navigation, and structural analysis.

Sine Rule

$$ \frac{a}{\sin A} = \frac{b}{\sin B} = \frac{c}{\sin C} $$

Use when: You know two angles and one side, or two sides and a non-included angle.

Cosine Rule

For sides: \( a^2 = b^2 + c^2 - 2bc\cos A \)

For angles: \( \cos A = \frac{b^2 + c^2 - a^2}{2bc} \)

Use when: You know three sides, or two sides and the included angle.

Example: Triangulation Survey

Two survey points are 100 m apart. From point A, a third point C is at 40°; from point B, point C is at 65°. Find distances AC and BC.

Step 1: Find angle at C
\( \angle C = 180^\circ - 40^\circ - 65^\circ = 75^\circ \)

Step 2: Apply sine rule
\( \frac{AC}{\sin 65^\circ} = \frac{BC}{\sin 40^\circ} = \frac{100}{\sin 75^\circ} \)

Step 3: Calculate AC
\( AC = \frac{100 \times \sin 65^\circ}{\sin 75^\circ} = \frac{100 \times 0.9063}{0.9659} = 93.8 \text{ m} \)

Step 4: Calculate BC
\( BC = \frac{100 \times \sin 40^\circ}{\sin 75^\circ} = \frac{100 \times 0.6428}{0.9659} = 66.5 \text{ m} \)

Engineering Interpretation: The third point is 93.8 m from A and 66.5 m from B.

4. Trigonometric Identities

Algebraic Manipulation of Trigonometric Expressions

Identities simplify complex trigonometric expressions and solve equations that appear in wave analysis, signal processing, and vibration analysis.

Fundamental Identities

Pythagorean: \( \sin^2\theta + \cos^2\theta = 1 \)

Reciprocal: \( \csc\theta = \frac{1}{\sin\theta}, \quad \sec\theta = \frac{1}{\cos\theta}, \quad \cot\theta = \frac{1}{\tan\theta} \)

Quotient: \( \tan\theta = \frac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta} \)

Compound Angle Formulas

Sum/Difference: \( \sin(A \pm B) = \sin A\cos B \pm \cos A\sin B \)

Sum/Difference: \( \cos(A \pm B) = \cos A\cos B \mp \sin A\sin B \)

Double Angle Formulas

Sine: \( \sin 2A = 2\sin A\cos A \)

Cosine: \( \cos 2A = \cos^2 A - \sin^2 A = 2\cos^2 A - 1 = 1 - 2\sin^2 A \)

Example: Signal Analysis

Simplify the expression \( \sin(3x)\cos(2x) - \cos(3x)\sin(2x) \) using trigonometric identities.

Step 1: Recognize the pattern
This matches the form \( \sin A\cos B - \cos A\sin B \) where \( A = 3x \) and \( B = 2x \)

Step 2: Apply sine difference formula
\( \sin A\cos B - \cos A\sin B = \sin(A - B) \)

Step 3: Substitute values
\( \sin(3x)\cos(2x) - \cos(3x)\sin(2x) = \sin(3x - 2x) = \sin x \)

Engineering Interpretation: The complex expression simplifies to a basic sine wave, revealing the fundamental frequency component.

5. Harmonic Form and Wave Combination

Analyzing Combined Oscillations

The R-α method combines multiple sinusoidal waves of the same frequency, essential for analyzing interference patterns in AC circuits, mechanical vibrations, and wave phenomena.

R-α Method

Express \( a\sin\theta + b\cos\theta \) as \( R\sin(\theta + \alpha) \) or \( R\cos(\theta - \alpha) \):

Amplitude: \( R = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2} \)

Phase Angle: \( \tan\alpha = \frac{b}{a} \) (adjust quadrant based on signs of a and b)

Example: AC Circuit Analysis

Two voltage sources in a circuit: \( V_1 = 120\sin(\omega t) \) and \( V_2 = 90\cos(\omega t) \). Find the resultant voltage in harmonic form.

Step 1: Express in standard form
\( V_{total} = 120\sin(\omega t) + 90\cos(\omega t) \)
Here, \( a = 120 \), \( b = 90 \)

Step 2: Calculate resultant amplitude
\( R = \sqrt{120^2 + 90^2} = \sqrt{14400 + 8100} = \sqrt{22500} = 150 \)

Step 3: Calculate phase angle
\( \tan\alpha = \frac{90}{120} = 0.75 \)
\( \alpha = \tan^{-1}(0.75) \approx 0.6435 \text{ rad} \ (36.87^\circ) \)

Step 4: Write harmonic form
\( V_{total} = 150\sin(\omega t + 0.6435) \)

Engineering Interpretation: The combined voltage has amplitude 150 V and leads the original sine wave by 36.87°.

6. Engineering Applications

Real-World Trigonometric Problem Solving

Trigonometric principles find extensive application across all engineering disciplines for analysis, design, and problem-solving.

Mechanical Engineering Applications

  • Force Analysis: Resolving forces into components
  • Vibration Analysis: Modeling oscillatory motion
  • Mechanism Design: Analyzing linkage movements
  • Gear Systems: Calculating angular velocities

Electrical Engineering Applications

  • AC Circuit Analysis: Phasor diagrams and impedance
  • Signal Processing: Fourier analysis and filtering
  • Power Systems: Three-phase power calculations
  • Communications: Modulation and demodulation

Civil Engineering Applications

  • Surveying: Triangulation and distance measurement
  • Structural Analysis: Truss forces and beam deflection
  • Construction: Slope stability and earthworks
  • Hydraulics: Flow direction and velocity components

Example: Roof Truss Analysis

A symmetrical roof truss has a span of 10 m and height of 2.5 m. Calculate the length of the rafters and the angle at the peak.

Step 1: Analyze half the truss
This creates a right triangle with base = 5 m, height = 2.5 m

Step 2: Calculate rafter length (hypotenuse)
\( L = \sqrt{5^2 + 2.5^2} = \sqrt{25 + 6.25} = \sqrt{31.25} = 5.59 \text{ m} \)

Step 3: Calculate peak angle
\( \tan\theta = \frac{2.5}{5} = 0.5 \)
\( \theta = \tan^{-1}(0.5) = 26.57^\circ \)
Total peak angle = \( 2 \times 26.57^\circ = 53.14^\circ \)

Engineering Interpretation: Each rafter is 5.59 m long, and the roof has a 53.14° peak angle.

Engineering Practice: Trigonometric analysis is not just about mathematical manipulation but understanding the physical significance of angles, distances, and periodic behavior in engineering systems. Always interpret results in the context of the specific application.
