AC Circuit Analysis

Engineering Application of Complex Numbers

Complex numbers provide an elegant mathematical framework for analyzing AC circuits. By representing sinusoidal quantities as phasors (complex numbers with magnitude and phase), we transform differential equations into algebraic equations, significantly simplifying circuit analysis.

1. AC Fundamentals and Waveform Characteristics

Sinusoidal Waveforms

Alternating Current (AC) systems are dominated by sinusoidal waveforms due to their mathematical properties and practical generation through rotating machinery. The general form of a sinusoidal voltage or current is:
$$ v(t) = V_m\sin(\omega t + \phi) \quad \text{or} \quad v(t) = V_m\cos(\omega t + \phi) $$

Where:

  • \( V_m \) = Peak amplitude (volts or amperes)
  • \( \omega \) = Angular frequency (radians/second)
  • \( t \) = Time (seconds)
  • \( \phi \) = Phase angle (radians)

Key AC Parameters

Parameter Symbol Formula Description
Frequency \( f \) \( f = \frac{\omega}{2\pi} \) Number of cycles per second (Hz)
Period \( T \) \( T = \frac{1}{f} = \frac{2\pi}{\omega} \) Time for one complete cycle (seconds)
Angular Frequency \( \omega \) \( \omega = 2\pi f \) Radians per second
Peak Value \( V_m \) or \( I_m \) Maximum amplitude Maximum instantaneous value
RMS Value \( V_{rms} \) or \( I_{rms} \) \( V_{rms} = \frac{V_m}{\sqrt{2}} \) DC equivalent for power calculation
Peak-to-Peak \( V_{pp} \) or \( I_{pp} \) \( V_{pp} = 2V_m \) Difference between maximum and minimum
Engineering Note: Unless specified otherwise, AC voltages and currents are typically quoted as RMS values. For example, "230V AC" means 230V RMS, with a peak value of approximately 325V.

Example: Complete AC Waveform Analysis

An AC voltage is given by \( v(t) = 120\sin(100\pi t + \frac{\pi}{4}) \) volts. Determine all key parameters.

Solution:

a) Peak Voltage: \( V_m = 120 \) V

b) RMS Voltage: \( V_{rms} = \frac{120}{\sqrt{2}} \approx 84.85 \) V

c) Angular Frequency: \( \omega = 100\pi \approx 314.16 \) rad/s

d) Frequency: \( f = \frac{\omega}{2\pi} = \frac{100\pi}{2\pi} = 50 \) Hz

e) Period: \( T = \frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{50} = 0.02 \) s = 20 ms

f) Phase Angle: \( \phi = \frac{\pi}{4} = 45^\circ \) (voltage leads reference)

g) Instantaneous voltage at t=10ms: \( v(0.01) = 120\sin(100\pi \times 0.01 + \frac{\pi}{4}) = 120\sin(\pi + \frac{\pi}{4}) = 120\sin(\frac{5\pi}{4}) \approx -84.85 \) V

2. Phasor Representation of AC Quantities

Phasor Domain Transformation

Phasors convert time-domain sinusoidal functions into complex numbers in the frequency domain. This transformation eliminates the time variable, simplifying analysis.

Phasor Conversion: A sinusoidal function \( v(t) = V_m\cos(\omega t + \phi) \) is represented as a phasor \( \mathbf{V} = V_{rms} \angle \phi \).

$$ v(t) = V_m\cos(\omega t + \phi) \quad \Leftrightarrow \quad \mathbf{V} = V_{rms} \angle \phi = V_{rms} e^{j\phi} $$

Complex Number Forms

Form Expression Conversion
Rectangular \( a + jb \) \( a = |Z|\cos\theta, \quad b = |Z|\sin\theta \)
Polar \( |Z| \angle \theta \) \( |Z| = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}, \quad \theta = \tan^{-1}(\frac{b}{a}) \)
Exponential \( |Z| e^{j\theta} \) Euler's formula: \( e^{j\theta} = \cos\theta + j\sin\theta \)

Example: Phasor Operations

Convert the following time-domain functions to phasor form:

a) \( i_1(t) = 10\cos(377t + 30^\circ) \) A

b) \( v_2(t) = 5\sin(100\pi t - 45^\circ) \) V (convert to cosine first)

Solution:

a) \( I_1 = \frac{10}{\sqrt{2}} \angle 30^\circ \approx 7.07 \angle 30^\circ \) A

b) First convert to cosine: \( \sin(\theta) = \cos(\theta - 90^\circ) \)

\( v_2(t) = 5\cos(100\pi t - 45^\circ - 90^\circ) = 5\cos(100\pi t - 135^\circ) \)

\( V_2 = \frac{5}{\sqrt{2}} \angle -135^\circ \approx 3.54 \angle -135^\circ \) V

3. Impedance and Admittance

Generalized Resistance

Impedance (Z) extends the concept of resistance to AC circuits, accounting for both magnitude and phase relationships. Admittance (Y) is the reciprocal of impedance, particularly useful for parallel circuit analysis.

Component Impedance Characteristics

Component Impedance (Z) Magnitude Phase Angle Power Dissipation
Resistor (R) \( Z_R = R \) \( R \) \( 0^\circ \) Dissipates real power
Inductor (L) \( Z_L = j\omega L \) \( \omega L \) \( +90^\circ \) Stores energy in magnetic field
Capacitor (C) \( Z_C = \frac{1}{j\omega C} = -j\frac{1}{\omega C} \) \( \frac{1}{\omega C} \) \( -90^\circ \) Stores energy in electric field
Memory Aid: "ELI the ICE man" - E (voltage) leads I (current) in L (inductor), I leads E in C (capacitor).

Example: Series RLC Circuit Analysis

A series RLC circuit has R = 100Ω, L = 0.5H, C = 10μF, connected to a 50Hz, 230V RMS supply. Calculate the total impedance, current, and voltage across each component.

Solution:

Step 1: Calculate angular frequency
\( \omega = 2\pi f = 2\pi(50) = 100\pi \approx 314.16 \) rad/s

Step 2: Calculate individual impedances
\( Z_R = 100 \) Ω
\( Z_L = j\omega L = j(314.16)(0.5) = j157.08 \) Ω
\( Z_C = -j\frac{1}{\omega C} = -j\frac{1}{314.16 \times 10 \times 10^{-6}} = -j318.31 \) Ω

Step 3: Total impedance
\( Z_{total} = Z_R + Z_L + Z_C = 100 + j157.08 - j318.31 = 100 - j161.23 \) Ω

Magnitude: \( |Z| = \sqrt{100^2 + (-161.23)^2} \approx 189.47 \) Ω

Phase: \( \theta = \tan^{-1}(\frac{-161.23}{100}) \approx -58.17^\circ \)

Step 4: Circuit current
\( I = \frac{V}{Z} = \frac{230\angle 0^\circ}{189.47\angle -58.17^\circ} \approx 1.214\angle 58.17^\circ \) A

Step 5: Component voltages
\( V_R = I \times R = 1.214\angle 58.17^\circ \times 100 \approx 121.4\angle 58.17^\circ \) V
\( V_L = I \times Z_L = 1.214\angle 58.17^\circ \times 157.08\angle 90^\circ \approx 190.7\angle 148.17^\circ \) V
\( V_C = I \times Z_C = 1.214\angle 58.17^\circ \times 318.31\angle -90^\circ \approx 386.4\angle -31.83^\circ \) V
