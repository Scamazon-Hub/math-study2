Differential Equations - Modeling Dynamic Systems

Differential Equations

The Language of Change in Engineering Systems

Differential equations provide the mathematical framework for modeling dynamic systems across all engineering disciplines, from electrical circuits and mechanical vibrations to heat transfer and fluid dynamics.

1. Introduction to Ordinary Differential Equations (ODEs)

Classifying Differential Equations

Understanding the type and order of a differential equation determines the appropriate solution method and provides insight into the system's behavior.

Classification of ODEs

Classification Definition Example Engineering Application
Order Highest derivative in equation \( \frac{d^2y}{dt^2} + 3\frac{dy}{dt} + 2y = 0 \) (2nd order) Mass-spring-damper systems
Linearity Linear in dependent variable and derivatives \( y'' + p(t)y' + q(t)y = g(t) \) (linear) Linear circuit analysis
Homogeneity All terms contain dependent variable or derivatives \( y'' + 4y' + 3y = 0 \) (homogeneous) Free vibrations

Engineering Applications of ODEs

Electrical Circuits: \( L\frac{d^2i}{dt^2} + R\frac{di}{dt} + \frac{1}{C}i = \frac{dV}{dt} \)

Mechanical Vibrations: \( m\frac{d^2x}{dt^2} + c\frac{dx}{dt} + kx = F(t) \)

Heat Transfer: \( \frac{dT}{dt} = k(T - T_{env}) \)

Population Dynamics: \( \frac{dP}{dt} = kP\left(1 - \frac{P}{K}\right) \)

2. First-Order Linear ODEs

Solving First-Order Systems

First-order linear ODEs model systems with exponential growth/decay, RC circuits, and simple thermal systems. The integrating factor method provides a systematic solution approach.

Standard Form and Integrating Factor

Standard Form: \( \frac{dy}{dt} + P(t)y = Q(t) \)

Integrating Factor: \( \mu(t) = e^{\int P(t) dt} \)

Solution: \( y(t) = \frac{1}{\mu(t)} \left[ \int \mu(t) Q(t) dt + C \right] \)

Example: RC Circuit Analysis

Solve the RC circuit equation: \( \frac{dv}{dt} + \frac{1}{RC}v = \frac{V_s}{RC} \) with \( v(0) = 0 \).

Step 1: Identify P(t) and Q(t)
\( P(t) = \frac{1}{RC} \) (constant), \( Q(t) = \frac{V_s}{RC} \)

Step 2: Calculate integrating factor
\( \mu(t) = e^{\int \frac{1}{RC} dt} = e^{\frac{t}{RC}} \)

Step 3: Multiply through by integrating factor
\( e^{\frac{t}{RC}}\frac{dv}{dt} + \frac{1}{RC}e^{\frac{t}{RC}}v = \frac{V_s}{RC}e^{\frac{t}{RC}} \)
The left side is \( \frac{d}{dt}\left(ve^{\frac{t}{RC}}\right) \)

Step 4: Integrate both sides
\( ve^{\frac{t}{RC}} = \int \frac{V_s}{RC}e^{\frac{t}{RC}} dt = V_s e^{\frac{t}{RC}} + C \)

Step 5: Solve for v(t)
\( v(t) = V_s + Ce^{-\frac{t}{RC}} \)

Step 6: Apply initial condition
\( v(0) = V_s + C = 0 \Rightarrow C = -V_s \)
\( v(t) = V_s(1 - e^{-\frac{t}{RC}}) \)

Circuit Design: This solution describes capacitor charging in RC circuits, with time constant τ = RC.

3. Second-Order Linear ODEs

Modeling Oscillatory Systems

Second-order ODEs describe systems with inertia, stiffness, and damping, such as mechanical vibrations, electrical circuits with inductance, and structural dynamics.

Homogeneous Solutions

Standard Form: \( a\frac{d^2y}{dt^2} + b\frac{dy}{dt} + cy = 0 \)

Characteristic Equation: \( ar^2 + br + c = 0 \)

Roots: \( r_{1,2} = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a} \)

Solution Cases

Case Condition General Solution Engineering Interpretation
Distinct Real Roots \( b^2 - 4ac > 0 \) \( y(t) = C_1e^{r_1t} + C_2e^{r_2t} \) Overdamped system
Repeated Real Roots \( b^2 - 4ac = 0 \) \( y(t) = (C_1 + C_2t)e^{rt} \) Critically damped system
Complex Roots \( b^2 - 4ac < 0 \) \( y(t) = e^{\alpha t}(C_1\cos\beta t + C_2\sin\beta t) \) Underdamped oscillations

Example: Mass-Spring-Damper System

Solve \( \frac{d^2x}{dt^2} + 4\frac{dx}{dt} + 3x = 0 \) with \( x(0) = 1 \), \( x'(0) = 0 \).

Step 1: Characteristic equation
\( r^2 + 4r + 3 = 0 \)

Step 2: Find roots
\( (r + 1)(r + 3) = 0 \Rightarrow r = -1, -3 \)

Step 3: General solution
\( x(t) = C_1e^{-t} + C_2e^{-3t} \)

Step 4: Apply initial conditions
\( x(0) = C_1 + C_2 = 1 \)
\( x'(t) = -C_1e^{-t} - 3C_2e^{-3t} \Rightarrow x'(0) = -C_1 - 3C_2 = 0 \)

Step 5: Solve for constants
From second equation: \( C_1 = -3C_2 \)
Substitute into first: \( -3C_2 + C_2 = 1 \Rightarrow -2C_2 = 1 \Rightarrow C_2 = -0.5 \)
Then \( C_1 = 1.5 \)

Step 6: Final solution
\( x(t) = 1.5e^{-t} - 0.5e^{-3t} \)

Mechanical Analysis: This represents an overdamped system with no oscillations.

Non-Homogeneous Equations and Particular Solutions

Solving Forced Systems

Non-homogeneous ODEs model systems with external forcing functions. The method of undetermined coefficients provides solutions for common forcing functions.

Method of Undetermined Coefficients

Forcing Function g(t) Particular Solution Form Example
Constant (k) A (constant) \( y_p = A \)
Polynomial (tⁿ) Polynomial of same degree \( y_p = At^2 + Bt + C \) for t²
Exponential (e^{at}) Ae^{at} \( y_p = Ae^{3t} \) for e^{3t}
Sine/Cosine (sin(ωt) or cos(ωt)) Acos(ωt) + Bsin(ωt) \( y_p = Acos(2t) + Bsin(2t) \) for sin(2t)

Example: Forced Vibrations

Solve \( \frac{d^2x}{dt^2} + 4x = 8\sin(2t) \) with \( x(0) = 0 \), \( x'(0) = 0 \).

Step 1: Homogeneous solution
\( r^2 + 4 = 0 \Rightarrow r = \pm 2i \)
\( x_h(t) = C_1\cos(2t) + C_2\sin(2t) \)

Step 2: Particular solution
Since sin(2t) is already in homogeneous solution, use:
\( x_p(t) = t(A\cos(2t) + B\sin(2t)) \)

Step 3: Differentiate and substitute
\( x_p' = A\cos(2t) + B\sin(2t) + t(-2A\sin(2t) + 2B\cos(2t)) \)
\( x_p'' = -4A\sin(2t) + 4B\cos(2t) + t(-4A\cos(2t) - 4B\sin(2t)) \)
Substitute into ODE and solve for A and B

Step 4: Apply initial conditions
After solving, we get: \( x(t) = -\frac{1}{2}t\cos(2t) + \frac{1}{4}\sin(2t) \)

Vibration Analysis: This represents resonance with amplitude growing linearly with time.

4. Systems of Differential Equations

Coupled Dynamic Systems

Many engineering systems involve multiple interacting variables described by systems of differential equations, which can be solved using eigenvalue methods or numerical techniques.

Matrix Formulation

System of First-Order ODEs: \( \frac{d\mathbf{x}}{dt} = A\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{f}(t) \)

Where \( \mathbf{x} = \begin{pmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \\ \vdots \\ x_n \end{pmatrix} \), \( A \) is an n×n matrix, and \( \mathbf{f}(t) \) is a forcing vector.

Example: Coupled Spring-Mass System

Two masses connected by springs:

\( m_1\frac{d^2x_1}{dt^2} = -k_1x_1 + k_2(x_2 - x_1) \)
\( m_2\frac{d^2x_2}{dt^2} = -k_2(x_2 - x_1) \)

Step 1: Convert to first-order system
Let \( y_1 = x_1 \), \( y_2 = \frac{dx_1}{dt} \), \( y_3 = x_2 \), \( y_4 = \frac{dx_2}{dt} \)
Then:
\( \frac{dy_1}{dt} = y_2 \)
\( \frac{dy_2}{dt} = -\frac{k_1+k_2}{m_1}y_1 + \frac{k_2}{m_1}y_3 \)
\( \frac{dy_3}{dt} = y_4 \)
\( \frac{dy_4}{dt} = \frac{k_2}{m_2}y_1 - \frac{k_2}{m_2}y_3 \)

Step 2: Matrix form
\( \frac{d}{dt}\begin{pmatrix} y_1 \\ y_2 \\ y_3 \\ y_4 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 & 0 & 0 \\ -\frac{k_1+k_2}{m_1} & 0 & \frac{k_2}{m_1} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 \\ \frac{k_2}{m_2} & 0 & -\frac{k_2}{m_2} & 0 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} y_1 \\ y_2 \\ y_3 \\ y_4 \end{pmatrix} \)

Step 3: Solve using eigenvalues/eigenvectors
The natural frequencies are given by the eigenvalues of the coefficient matrix.

Mechanical Design: This analysis determines the vibration modes of coupled systems.

5. Numerical Methods for ODEs

Computational Solutions

When analytical solutions are unavailable or impractical, numerical methods provide approximate solutions to differential equations, essential for complex engineering systems.

Comparison of Numerical Methods

Method Formula Accuracy Stability Application
Euler's Method \( y_{n+1} = y_n + hf(t_n, y_n) \) O(h) Conditional Simple systems, education
Improved Euler \( y_{n+1} = y_n + \frac{h}{2}[f(t_n,y_n) + f(t_{n+1}, y_n + hf(t_n,y_n))] \) O(h²) Better Moderate accuracy needs
Runge-Kutta 4th Order \( y_{n+1} = y_n + \frac{h}{6}(k_1 + 2k_2 + 2k_3 + k_4) \) O(h⁴) Good High accuracy requirements

6. Engineering Applications

Real-World Differential Equation Problems

Differential equations model diverse engineering phenomena, from electrical circuits and control systems to fluid dynamics and structural analysis.

Application Areas

Engineering Field Typical ODE Parameters Solution Method
Electrical \( L\frac{d^2i}{dt^2} + R\frac{di}{dt} + \frac{1}{C}i = V(t) \) L, R, C, V(t) Characteristic equation, undetermined coefficients
Mechanical \( m\frac{d^2x}{dt^2} + c\frac{dx}{dt} + kx = F(t) \) m, c, k, F(t) Homogeneous + particular solutions
Thermal \( \frac{dT}{dt} = k(T - T_{env}) \) k, Tenv Separation of variables
Chemical \( \frac{dC}{dt} = -kC \) k (rate constant) Exponential solution

Example: RLC Circuit Analysis

An RLC circuit with L = 0.1 H, R = 10 Ω, C = 0.001 F, and V(t) = 100sin(100t) V. Find the current i(t).

Step 1: Circuit equation
\( 0.1\frac{d^2i}{dt^2} + 10\frac{di}{dt} + 1000i = 100\cos(100t) \)

Step 2: Homogeneous solution
Characteristic equation: \( 0.1r^2 + 10r + 1000 = 0 \)
Roots: \( r = -50 \pm j86.6 \) (underdamped)
\( i_h(t) = e^{-50t}(A\cos(86.6t) + B\sin(86.6t)) \)

Step 3: Particular solution
Assume \( i_p(t) = C\cos(100t) + D\sin(100t) \)
Substitute and solve for C and D

Step 4: Complete solution
\( i(t) = i_h(t) + i_p(t) \)

Circuit Design: The transient response decays quickly, leaving the steady-state sinusoidal response.

Engineering Practice: Differential equations are not just mathematical abstractions but essential tools for modeling and analyzing dynamic systems. The ability to formulate, solve, and interpret differential equations is fundamental to engineering design and analysis across all disciplines.

Differential Equations

The Language of Change in Engineering Systems

Differential equations provide the mathematical framework for modeling dynamic systems across all engineering disciplines, from electrical circuits and mechanical vibrations to heat transfer and fluid dynamics.

1. Introduction to Ordinary Differential Equations (ODEs)

Classifying Differential Equations

Understanding the type and order of a differential equation is the first step in determining the correct method to solve it and in understanding the physical system it represents.

Key Classifications

Classification Definition Example Engineering Context
Order The highest derivative present in the equation. \( \frac{d^2y}{dt^2} + 3\frac{dy}{dt} + 2y = 0 \) is 2nd order. Order often relates to the number of energy storage elements (e.g., an RLC circuit is 2nd order).
Linearity The equation is linear in the dependent variable (e.g., y) and its derivatives. \( y'' + t^2y' + y = \sin(t) \) is linear. Most introductory engineering systems are modeled with linear ODEs.
Homogeneity A linear equation is homogeneous if the right-hand side is zero. \( y'' + 4y' + 3y = 0 \) is homogeneous. Represents the natural response of a system (free vibrations, unpowered circuits).
Non-Homogeneous A linear equation with a non-zero function on the right-hand side. \( y'' + 4y' + 3y = \cos(t) \) is non-homogeneous. Represents a system with an external forcing function (forced vibrations, powered circuits).

2. First-Order Linear ODEs

Solving Systems with One Energy Storage Element

First-order ODEs model systems like RC/RL circuits, radioactive decay, and simple thermal systems. The standard solution method involves using an integrating factor.

Solution via Integrating Factor

For an ODE in the standard form: \( \frac{dy}{dt} + P(t)y = Q(t) \)

1. Find the Integrating Factor (\(\mu(t)\)): \( \mu(t) = e^{\int P(t) dt} \)

2. The solution is given by: \( y(t) = \frac{1}{\mu(t)} \left[ \int \mu(t) Q(t) dt + C \right] \)

Example: RL Circuit Analysis

For an RL circuit, Kirchhoff's Voltage Law gives \( L\frac{di}{dt} + Ri = V_s \). Find the current \(i(t)\) if \(i(0) = 0\).

Step 1: Put into standard form
\( \frac{di}{dt} + \frac{R}{L}i = \frac{V_s}{L} \). Here, \( P(t) = \frac{R}{L} \) and \( Q(t) = \frac{V_s}{L} \).

Step 2: Find the integrating factor
\( \mu(t) = e^{\int \frac{R}{L} dt} = e^{\frac{R}{L}t} \)

Step 3: Apply the solution formula
\( i(t) = \frac{1}{e^{\frac{R}{L}t}} \left[ \int e^{\frac{R}{L}t} \cdot \frac{V_s}{L} dt + C \right] \)
\( i(t) = e^{-\frac{R}{L}t} \left[ \frac{V_s}{L} \cdot \frac{L}{R} e^{\frac{R}{L}t} + C \right] = \frac{V_s}{R} + Ce^{-\frac{R}{L}t} \)

Step 4: Apply the initial condition \(i(0)=0\)
\( 0 = \frac{V_s}{R} + C \cdot e^0 \implies C = -\frac{V_s}{R} \)

Final Solution: \( i(t) = \frac{V_s}{R}(1 - e^{-\frac{R}{L}t}) \)

Engineering Interpretation: The current starts at 0 and exponentially rises to a steady-state value of \(V_s/R\), with a time constant of \(\tau = L/R\).

3. Second-Order Linear Homogeneous ODEs

Modeling Vibrations and Oscillations

These equations are fundamental to analyzing RLC circuits and mass-spring-damper systems. The solution depends entirely on the roots of the characteristic equation.

The Characteristic Equation Method

For an ODE of the form: \( a\frac{d^2y}{dt^2} + b\frac{dy}{dt} + cy = 0 \)

The Characteristic Equation is: \( ar^2 + br + c = 0 \)

The solution for \(y(t)\) depends on the roots \(r_1, r_2\) of this quadratic equation.

The Three Cases for Solutions

Case Discriminant (\(b^2 - 4ac\)) Nature of Roots General Solution \(y(t)\) Engineering Interpretation
1 \( > 0 \) Two distinct real roots (\(r_1, r_2\)) \( C_1e^{r_1t} + C_2e^{r_2t} \) Overdamped: System returns to equilibrium slowly without oscillation.
2 \( = 0 \) One repeated real root (\(r\)) \( (C_1 + C_2t)e^{rt} \) Critically Damped: Fastest return to equilibrium without oscillation.
3 \( < 0 \) Complex conjugate roots (\(\alpha \pm j\beta\)) \( e^{\alpha t}(C_1\cos(\beta t) + C_2\sin(\beta t)) \) Underdamped: System oscillates with decreasing amplitude.

4. Non-Homogeneous ODEs (Forced Systems)

Analyzing Systems with External Inputs

When a system is subjected to an external force or input (e.g., an AC voltage source, an external vibrating force), the equation becomes non-homogeneous. The general solution is the sum of the homogeneous solution and a particular solution.
$$ y(t) = y_h(t) + y_p(t) $$

\(y_h(t)\): The homogeneous solution (transient response) which describes the system's natural behavior and dies out over time.

\(y_p(t)\): The particular solution (steady-state response) which describes the system's long-term behavior under the external forcing function.
