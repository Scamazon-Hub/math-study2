A Note on Units & Notation

Why Units Matter

In engineering, a number without a unit is meaningless. Using consistent **SI (Système International)** units is essential to avoid costly errors. Before substituting values into any formula, always convert all quantities to their base SI units (e.g., convert km/h to m/s, or mm to m).

2. Common SI Prefixes

Making Numbers Manageable

Prefixes are used to represent very large or very small quantities conveniently. Always convert values to their base unit (without a prefix) before using them in a formula.
PrefixSymbolMultiplier
gigaG$$10^9$$ (billion)
megaM$$10^6$$ (million)
kilok$$10^3$$ (thousand)
millim$$10^{-3}$$ (thousandth)
micro$$\mu$$$$10^{-6}$$ (millionth)
nanon$$10^{-9}$$ (billionth)

Example: Convert 250 milliamps (mA) to amps (A) and 0.05 megaohms (M$$\Omega$$) to ohms ($$\Omega$$).

$$ 250 \text{ mA} = 250 \times 10^{-3} \text{ A} = 0.25 \text{ A} $$

$$ 0.05 \text{ M}\Omega = 0.05 \times 10^6 \, \Omega = 50,000 \, \Omega \text{ (or } 50 \text{ k}\Omega) $$

3. A Note on Notation

Understanding Negative Exponents

The notation with a negative exponent is an alternative way of writing "per". They mean the same thing and are used interchangeably.
  • $$ ms^{-1} $$ is the same as "metres per second" or $$ \frac{m}{s} $$.
  • $$ ms^{-2} $$ is the same as "metres per second squared" or $$ \frac{m}{s^2} $$.
