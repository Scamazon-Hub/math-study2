The Inverse Approach - Integration by Reversing Differentiation

The Inverse Approach to Integration

Reversing the Differentiation Process

The inverse approach treats integration as the reverse operation of differentiation. This powerful method allows us to derive integration formulas by asking: "What function, when differentiated, gives us this result?"

1. Fundamental Concept

Integration as the Inverse of Differentiation

Just as subtraction reverses addition and division reverses multiplication, integration reverses differentiation. This relationship forms the foundation for deriving integration rules.

The Basic Principle

If differentiation transforms function \(f(x)\) into \(f'(x)\), then integration transforms \(f'(x)\) back to \(f(x)\):

\[ \frac{d}{dx}[f(x)] = f'(x) \quad \Rightarrow \quad \int f'(x) \, dx = f(x) + c \]

where \(c\) is the constant of integration.

Advantages of the Inverse Approach

  • Simple to apply: No new concepts or complex proofs required
  • Self-checking: Verify results by differentiating them
  • Intuitive: Builds on existing knowledge of differentiation

2. The Special Case of 1/x

Avoiding the Blind Alley

The power rule for integration fails when \(n = -1\), requiring an alternative approach using logarithmic functions.

The Problem with the Power Rule

Attempting to apply the power rule to \(1/x\) leads to an undefined result:

\[ \int x^{-1} \, dx = \frac{x^0}{0} + c \quad \text{(undefined)} \]

Important: The standard power rule \(\int ax^n \, dx = \frac{ax^{n+1}}{n+1} + c\) only applies when \(n \neq -1\).

The Logarithmic Solution

Finding the Correct Primitive

We ask: "What function, when differentiated, gives \(\frac{1}{x}\)?"

Step 1: Recall derivative of ln(x)
\[ \frac{d}{dx}[\ln x] = \frac{1}{x} \]

Step 2: Apply the inverse principle
Since differentiation of \(\ln x\) gives \(\frac{1}{x}\), the inverse operation gives:
\[ \int \frac{1}{x} \, dx = \ln x + c \]

Step 3: Verification by differentiation
\[ \frac{d}{dx}[\ln x + c] = \frac{1}{x} \quad \checkmark \]

Extended Form: 1/(ax+b)

Using the chain rule, we can extend this to more general forms:

\[ \frac{d}{dx}[\ln(ax + b)] = \frac{1}{ax + b} \cdot a = \frac{a}{ax + b} \]

Reversing this gives the integration formula:

\[ \int \frac{1}{ax + b} \, dx = \frac{1}{a} \ln(ax + b) + c \]

3. Logarithmic Integration Examples

Practical Applications

These integration techniques are essential for solving problems involving rates that follow inverse relationships, common in physics and engineering.

Example 1: Basic Logarithmic Integration

Integrate \( f(x) = \frac{5}{x} \).

Step 1: Extract the constant
\[ I = \int \frac{5}{x} \, dx = 5 \int \frac{1}{x} \, dx \]

Step 2: Apply the integration rule
\[ I = 5 \ln x + c \]

Step 3: Verification
\[ \frac{d}{dx}[5 \ln x + c] = 5 \cdot \frac{1}{x} = \frac{5}{x} \quad \checkmark \]

Example 2: Definite Integral with Logarithm

Evaluate \( \int_{1}^{2} \frac{1}{5t} \, dt \).

Step 1: Extract the constant
\[ I = \int_{1}^{2} \frac{1}{5t} \, dt = \frac{1}{5} \int_{1}^{2} \frac{1}{t} \, dt \]

Step 2: Apply the integration rule
\[ I = \frac{1}{5} [\ln t]_{1}^{2} \]

Step 3: Evaluate at limits
\[ I = \frac{1}{5} (\ln 2 - \ln 1) = \frac{1}{5} (\ln 2 - 0) = \frac{\ln 2}{5} \]

Step 4: Numerical approximation
\[ I \approx \frac{0.6931}{5} \approx 0.139 \quad \text{(to 3 significant figures)} \]

Example 3: Generalized Form

Integrate \( f(x) = \frac{2}{3x-1} \).

Step 1: Identify a and b
Comparing with \( \frac{1}{ax+b} \), we have \( a = 3 \), \( b = -1 \)

Step 2: Extract the constant
\[ I = \int \frac{2}{3x-1} \, dx = 2 \int \frac{1}{3x-1} \, dx \]

Step 3: Apply the integration rule
\[ I = 2 \left[ \frac{1}{3} \ln(3x-1) \right] + c = \frac{2}{3} \ln(3x-1) + c \]

Step 4: Verification
\[ \frac{d}{dx} \left[ \frac{2}{3} \ln(3x-1) + c \right] = \frac{2}{3} \cdot \frac{3}{3x-1} = \frac{2}{3x-1} \quad \checkmark \]

4. Integration of Binomial Powers

Extending the Power Rule

The inverse approach allows us to derive integration formulas for expressions of the form \((ax+b)^n\), which frequently appear in engineering applications.

Derivation of the Formula

Step 1: Differentiate \((ax+b)^{n+1}\)
Using the chain rule:
\[ \frac{d}{dx}[(ax+b)^{n+1}] = (n+1)(ax+b)^n \cdot a = a(n+1)(ax+b)^n \]

Step 2: Apply the inverse principle
\[ \int a(n+1)(ax+b)^n \, dx = (ax+b)^{n+1} + c \]

Step 3: Solve for the integral
\[ \int (ax+b)^n \, dx = \frac{(ax+b)^{n+1}}{a(n+1)} + c \]

Example 4: Integrating a Binomial Power

Integrate \( 12(2x+1)^3 \).

Step 1: Identify parameters
\( a = 2 \), \( b = 1 \), \( n = 3 \)

Step 2: Extract the constant
\[ I = \int 12(2x+1)^3 \, dx = 12 \int (2x+1)^3 \, dx \]

Step 3: Apply the integration rule
\[ I = 12 \left[ \frac{(2x+1)^4}{2 \cdot 4} \right] + c = 12 \cdot \frac{(2x+1)^4}{8} + c \]

Step 4: Simplify
\[ I = 1.5(2x+1)^4 + c \]

Step 5: Verification
\[ \frac{d}{dx}[1.5(2x+1)^4 + c] = 1.5 \cdot 4(2x+1)^3 \cdot 2 = 12(2x+1)^3 \quad \checkmark \]

Example 5: Definite Integral with Fractional Power

Evaluate \( \int_{0}^{2} \frac{2}{\sqrt{4y+1}} \, dy \).

Step 1: Rewrite in index form
\[ I = \int_{0}^{2} 2(4y+1)^{-1/2} \, dy \]

Step 2: Extract the constant
\[ I = 2 \int_{0}^{2} (4y+1)^{-1/2} \, dy \]

Step 3: Identify parameters
\( a = 4 \), \( b = 1 \), \( n = -1/2 \)

Step 4: Apply the integration rule
\[ I = 2 \left[ \frac{(4y+1)^{1/2}}{4 \cdot (1/2)} \right]_{0}^{2} = 2 \left[ \frac{(4y+1)^{1/2}}{2} \right]_{0}^{2} = \left[ (4y+1)^{1/2} \right]_{0}^{2} \]

Step 5: Evaluate at limits
\[ I = \sqrt{4(2)+1} - \sqrt{4(0)+1} = \sqrt{9} - \sqrt{1} = 3 - 1 = 2 \]

5. Integration of Exponential Functions

Exponential Growth and Decay

Exponential functions model many natural phenomena, from radioactive decay to population growth, making their integration particularly important in engineering.

Derivation of the Formula

Step 1: Differentiate \(e^{ax+b}\)
Using the chain rule:
\[ \frac{d}{dx}[e^{ax+b}] = e^{ax+b} \cdot a = ae^{ax+b} \]

Step 2: Apply the inverse principle
\[ \int ae^{ax+b} \, dx = e^{ax+b} + c \]

Step 3: Solve for the integral
\[ \int e^{ax+b} \, dx = \frac{1}{a} e^{ax+b} + c \]

Example 6: Integrating an Exponential Function

Integrate \( f(x) = 3e^{(x/2)+1} \).

Step 1: Rewrite the exponent
\[ e^{(x/2)+1} = e^{(1/2)x+1} \]

Step 2: Extract the constant
\[ I = \int 3e^{(1/2)x+1} \, dx = 3 \int e^{(1/2)x+1} \, dx \]

Step 3: Identify parameters
\( a = 1/2 \), \( b = 1 \)

Step 4: Apply the integration rule
\[ I = 3 \left[ \frac{1}{1/2} e^{(1/2)x+1} \right] + c = 3 \cdot 2e^{(1/2)x+1} + c = 6e^{(x/2)+1} + c \]

Step 5: Verification
\[ \frac{d}{dx}[6e^{(x/2)+1} + c] = 6 \cdot e^{(x/2)+1} \cdot \frac{1}{2} = 3e^{(x/2)+1} \quad \checkmark \]

6. Additional Worked Examples

Consolidating the Concepts

These examples demonstrate the application of the inverse approach to various function types, reinforcing the techniques learned.

Example 7: Complex Fraction

Integrate \( f(x) = \frac{1}{2(3x-2)} \).

Step 1: Rewrite the function
\[ \frac{1}{2(3x-2)} = \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{3x-2} \]

Step 2: Extract the constant
\[ I = \int \frac{1}{2(3x-2)} \, dx = \frac{1}{2} \int \frac{1}{3x-2} \, dx \]

Step 3: Apply the integration rule
\[ I = \frac{1}{2} \left[ \frac{1}{3} \ln(3x-2) \right] + c = \frac{1}{6} \ln(3x-2) + c \]

Example 8: Definite Exponential Integral

Evaluate \( \int_{0}^{1} 4e^{2x} \, dx \).

Step 1: Extract the constant
\[ I = 4 \int_{0}^{1} e^{2x} \, dx \]

Step 2: Identify parameters
\( a = 2 \), \( b = 0 \)

Step 3: Apply the integration rule
\[ I = 4 \left[ \frac{1}{2} e^{2x} \right]_{0}^{1} = 2 [e^{2x}]_{0}^{1} \]

Step 4: Evaluate at limits
\[ I = 2(e^{2} - e^{0}) = 2(7.389 - 1) = 2(6.389) = 12.778 \approx 12.8 \]

Example 9: Variable Change

Integrate \( f(v) = (3v + 2)^3 \) with respect to \(v\).

Step 1: Identify parameters
\( a = 3 \), \( b = 2 \), \( n = 3 \)

Step 2: Apply the integration rule
\[ I = \int (3v + 2)^3 \, dv = \frac{(3v + 2)^4}{3 \cdot 4} + c = \frac{(3v + 2)^4}{12} + c \]

Example 10: Negative Exponent

Integrate \( f(t) = 3e^{(3-2t)} \).

Step 1: Rewrite the exponent
\[ e^{(3-2t)} = e^{-2t+3} \]

Step 2: Extract the constant
\[ I = \int 3e^{-2t+3} \, dt = 3 \int e^{-2t+3} \, dt \]

Step 3: Identify parameters
\( a = -2 \), \( b = 3 \)

Step 4: Apply the integration rule
\[ I = 3 \left[ \frac{1}{-2} e^{-2t+3} \right] + c = -1.5e^{(3-2t)} + c \]

Example 11: Definite Integral with Square Root

Evaluate \( \int_{1}^{2} 2\sqrt{7-3x} \, dx \).

Step 1: Rewrite in index form
\[ I = \int_{1}^{2} 2(7-3x)^{1/2} \, dx \]

Step 2: Extract the constant
\[ I = 2 \int_{1}^{2} (7-3x)^{1/2} \, dx \]

Step 3: Identify parameters
\( a = -3 \), \( b = 7 \), \( n = 1/2 \)

Step 4: Apply the integration rule
\[ I = 2 \left[ \frac{(7-3x)^{3/2}}{(-3) \cdot (3/2)} \right]_{1}^{2} = 2 \left[ \frac{(7-3x)^{3/2}}{-9/2} \right]_{1}^{2} = -\frac{4}{9} \left[ (7-3x)^{3/2} \right]_{1}^{2} \]

Step 5: Evaluate at limits
\[ I = -\frac{4}{9} \left[ (7-3(2))^{3/2} - (7-3(1))^{3/2} \right] = -\frac{4}{9} \left[ (1)^{3/2} - (4)^{3/2} \right] \]
\[ = -\frac{4}{9} (1 - 8) = -\frac{4}{9} (-7) = \frac{28}{9} \approx 3.11 \]

7. Integration Formulas Summary

Quick Reference Guide

This table summarizes the key integration formulas derived using the inverse approach, providing a handy reference for solving problems.
Function Type Function Integral Restrictions
Power Rule \( \int x^n \, dx \) \( \frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1} + c \) \( n \neq -1 \)
Logarithmic \( \int \frac{1}{x} \, dx \) \( \ln|x| + c \) \( x \neq 0 \)
General Logarithmic \( \int \frac{1}{ax+b} \, dx \) \( \frac{1}{a} \ln|ax+b| + c \) \( ax+b \neq 0 \)
Binomial Power \( \int (ax+b)^n \, dx \) \( \frac{(ax+b)^{n+1}}{a(n+1)} + c \) \( n \neq -1 \)
Exponential \( \int e^{ax+b} \, dx \) \( \frac{1}{a} e^{ax+b} + c \) \( a \neq 0 \)
Constant Multiple \( \int k \cdot f(x) \, dx \) \( k \cdot \int f(x) \, dx \) -
Sum/Difference \( \int [f(x) \pm g(x)] \, dx \) \( \int f(x) \, dx \pm \int g(x) \, dx \) -
Verification Strategy: Always verify your integration results by differentiating them. This self-checking property is one of the greatest strengths of the inverse approach to integration.
