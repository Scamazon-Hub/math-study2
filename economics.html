Financial & Engineering Economics

Economic Analysis for Engineering Decisions

Engineering economics involves the systematic evaluation of the economic merits of proposed solutions to engineering problems. It provides the analytical framework for making justifiable decisions regarding company assets, projects, and investments.

1. Fundamental Economic Principles

Core Concepts for Engineering Analysis

Understanding these fundamental principles is essential for making sound engineering economic decisions.

Time Value of Money

The principle that money available today is worth more than the same amount in the future due to its potential earning capacity.

Concept Formula Engineering Application
Present Value (PV) \( PV = \frac{FV}{(1 + i)^n} \) Evaluating future cash flows
Future Value (FV) \( FV = PV(1 + i)^n \) Projecting investment growth
Discount Rate Minimum acceptable rate of return Project selection criteria

Cost Concepts

Example: Cost Classification

For a manufacturing plant expansion project:

Fixed Costs: Land, building, permanent equipment
Variable Costs: Raw materials, utilities, labor
Direct Costs: Specific to the project
Indirect Costs: Overhead, administration
Sunk Costs: Already incurred (irrelevant to future decisions)

Engineering Insight: Only future costs that differ between alternatives are relevant for decision-making.

2. Depreciation Methods

Asset Value Allocation

Depreciation methods systematically allocate the cost of tangible assets over their useful lives, reflecting consumption of economic benefits.

Straight-Line Depreciation

When to Use

This is the simplest and most common method. Use when an asset's value is expected to decrease by a uniform amount each year.

Annual Depreciation (D): $$ D = \frac{\text{Cost} - \text{Salvage Value}}{\text{Useful Life}} $$

Book Value after n years: $$ BV_n = \text{Cost} - (n \times D) $$

Example: Manufacturing Equipment

A CNC machine costs £85,000, has a useful life of 8 years, and an estimated salvage value of £5,000.

Annual depreciation:
$$ D = \frac{85,000 - 5,000}{8} = \frac{80,000}{8} = \text{£10,000 per year} $$

Book value after 5 years:
$$ BV_5 = 85,000 - (5 \times 10,000) = \text{£35,000} $$

Engineering Application: Used for financial reporting and tax calculations where simplicity is preferred.

Reducing Balance Depreciation

When to Use

Use when an asset loses a larger proportion of its value in its earlier years. Depreciation is a fixed percentage of the asset's current book value.

Book Value after n years: $$ BV_n = \text{Cost} \times (1 - r)^n $$

Depreciation in year n: $$ D_n = BV_{n-1} \times r $$

Example: Company Vehicles

A delivery van costs £32,000 and depreciates at 25% per year using the reducing balance method.

Year 1: \( D_1 = 32,000 \times 0.25 = \text{£8,000} \), \( BV_1 = \text{£24,000} \)
Year 2: \( D_2 = 24,000 \times 0.25 = \text{£6,000} \), \( BV_2 = \text{£18,000} \)
Year 3: \( D_3 = 18,000 \times 0.25 = \text{£4,500} \), \( BV_3 = \text{£13,500} \)

Engineering Application: Better matches expense with revenue for assets that lose value rapidly initially.

Sum-of-the-Years'-Digits Depreciation

When to Use

This accelerated method results in higher depreciation charges in the early years, similar to reducing balance but with a different calculation approach.

Sum of digits for n years: $$ S = \frac{n(n+1)}{2} $$

Depreciation for year k: $$ D_k = (\text{Cost} - \text{Salvage}) \times \frac{n-k+1}{S} $$

Example: Specialized Testing Equipment

Equipment costs £60,000, 5-year life, £5,000 salvage value.

Sum of digits: $$ S = \frac{5(5+1)}{2} = 15 $$

Depreciable amount: \( 60,000 - 5,000 = \text{£55,000} \)

Year 1: \( D_1 = 55,000 \times \frac{5}{15} = \text{£18,333} \)
Year 2: \( D_2 = 55,000 \times \frac{4}{15} = \text{£14,667} \)
Year 3: \( D_3 = 55,000 \times \frac{3}{15} = \text{£11,000} \)

Tax Advantage: Higher early-year deductions reduce taxable income when the asset is most productive.

3. Interest Calculations

Quantifying the Time Value of Money

Interest calculations are fundamental to engineering economics, providing the mathematical basis for comparing cash flows occurring at different times.

Simple Interest

Future Value: $$ A = P(1 + rt) $$

Where: P = Principal, r = annual rate, t = time in years

Compound Interest

Annual compounding: $$ A = P(1 + r)^t $$

Continuous compounding: $$ A = Pe^{rt} $$

Multiple compounding periods per year: $$ A = P\left(1 + \frac{r}{m}\right)^{mt} $$

Example: Investment Comparison

Compare £10,000 invested for 5 years at 8% interest using different methods:

Simple Interest:
\( A = 10,000(1 + 0.08 \times 5) = \text{£14,000} \)

Compound Interest (annual):
\( A = 10,000(1.08)^5 = \text{£14,693.28} \)

Compound Interest (monthly):
\( A = 10,000\left(1 + \frac{0.08}{12}\right)^{60} = \text{£14,898.46} \)

Continuous Compounding:
\( A = 10,000e^{0.08 \times 5} = \text{£14,918.25} \)

Engineering Decision: The compounding frequency significantly affects the final value.

4. Investment Appraisal Techniques

Systematic Project Evaluation

These techniques provide objective criteria for evaluating the financial viability of engineering projects and investments.

Payback Period

Calculation: Time until cumulative cash inflows equal initial investment

Discounted Payback: Uses present values of cash flows

Net Present Value (NPV)

Formula: $$ NPV = \sum_{t=1}^{n} \frac{CF_t}{(1 + r)^t} - C_0 $$

Decision Rule: Accept if NPV > 0

Internal Rate of Return (IRR)

Definition: Discount rate that makes NPV = 0

Decision Rule: Accept if IRR > required rate of return

Example: Factory Expansion Project

Initial investment: £200,000, Required return: 12%, Project life: 5 years

5. Risk Analysis and Sensitivity

Accounting for Uncertainty

Engineering economic decisions involve uncertainty. Risk analysis techniques help quantify and manage this uncertainty.

Break-Even Analysis

Break-even point: $$ Q_{BE} = \frac{\text{Fixed Costs}}{\text{Price} - \text{Variable Cost per Unit}} $$

Sensitivity Analysis

Example: Production Facility

Fixed costs: £150,000/year, Variable cost: £25/unit, Selling price: £45/unit

Break-even quantity:
\( Q_{BE} = \frac{150,000}{45 - 25} = 7,500 \text{ units/year} \)

Sensitivity Analysis:
• 10% price decrease: \( Q_{BE} = \frac{150,000}{40.50 - 25} = 9,677 \text{ units} \) (+29%)
• 10% cost increase: \( Q_{BE} = \frac{150,000}{45 - 27.50} = 8,571 \text{ units} \) (+14%)
• 10% fixed cost increase: \( Q_{BE} = 8,250 \text{ units} \) (+10%)

Engineering Insight: The project is most sensitive to price changes.

6. Replacement Analysis

Optimal Equipment Replacement Timing

Determining the economically optimal time to replace aging equipment considering maintenance costs, efficiency, and technological changes.

Example: Fleet Vehicle Replacement

Current vehicle: Maintenance costs increasing by £500/year, current trade-in £8,000
New vehicle: Cost £35,000, lower operating costs, expected life 8 years

Economic Life Analysis:
Compare equivalent annual costs of keeping current vehicle vs. replacing

Decision Framework:
• Compare marginal cost of keeping vs. annual equivalent cost of new
• Consider technological improvements
• Factor in reliability and downtime costs

7. Practice Problems

Apply Your Knowledge

Work through these problems to reinforce your understanding of engineering economics principles.

Basic Problems

  1. Calculate the NPV of a project with initial cost £50,000 and annual cash flows of £15,000 for 5 years at 10% discount rate.
  2. A machine costs £40,000 with 5-year life and £5,000 salvage. Calculate depreciation using straight-line and reducing balance (30%) methods.
  3. Determine the break-even point for a product with fixed costs £80,000, variable cost £12/unit, selling price £25/unit.

Advanced Problems

  1. Compare two equipment options with different lives and costs using equivalent annual cost method.
  2. Perform sensitivity analysis on a project's NPV to changes in key assumptions.
  3. Calculate the internal rate of return for a project with irregular cash flows.
Engineering Economic Principle: The goal of engineering economics is not to minimize cost, but to maximize value. The best economic choice is the one that provides the greatest return relative to the resources invested.
